Community-Acquired Pneumonia (CAP)

Acute pulmonary parenchymal infection acquired outside the hospital, most commonly caused by S. pneumoniae.
#1 Infectious Cause of Death S. pneumoniae CURB-65 / PSI <1% outpatient → 50% ICU

Pathogenesis & Immune Response

Pneumonia develops through three primary routes: inhalation of aerosolized organisms (most common), microaspiration of oropharyngeal secretions, and rarely hematogenous spread (e.g., right-sided endocarditis seeding lungs with S. aureus).

Bacteria adhere to alveolar epithelial cells — S. pneumoniae uses phosphorylcholine to bind PAF receptor on type II pneumocytes. This triggers the innate immune cascade: alveolar macrophages recognize PAMPs via TLR-2 (gram-positives), TLR-4 (gram-negative LPS), releasing IL-1, IL-8, TNF-alpha. IL-8 drives massive neutrophil influx. Complement C3b enhances phagocytosis.

Inflammatory exudate (neutrophils, fibrin, RBCs, edema fluid) fills alveoli = consolidation on imaging, crackles on exam. Fluid-filled alveoli transmit sound better → bronchial breath sounds, egophony (E-to-A), increased tactile fremitus.

Key physiologic consequence: intrapulmonary shunt — blood perfuses consolidated non-ventilated alveoli → hypoxemia only partially responsive to supplemental O2 (distinguishes shunt from V/Q mismatch).

Classic Typical Pneumonia

  • Acute cough — productive in ~66%, purulent, rust-colored with S. pneumoniae
  • Fever — 80%; absent in 30% of elderly
  • Dyspnea — ~66%
  • Pleuritic chest pain — ~30%
  • Rigors / shaking chills — especially S. pneumoniae bacteremia (15–25%)

Physical Exam

Crackles (~80%), bronchial breath sounds, dullness to percussion, egophony (most specific for consolidation), increased tactile fremitus.

Elderly may present with confusion/delirium as the only sign — no fever, no cough. Immunocompromised may lack infiltrate on CXR.

CXR Findings

  • Lobar consolidation — classic bacterial (S. pneumoniae)
  • Air bronchograms — 99% specific for air-space disease
  • Bronchopneumonia — patchy, multifocal (S. aureus, H. flu)
  • Interstitial pattern — atypical organisms
  • Cavitation — Klebsiella, S. aureus, anaerobes, TB
  • Parapneumonic effusion — 20–40% of bacterial CAP. If >1 cm on lateral decubitus → sample it

  • Septic shock requiring vasopressors
  • Mechanical ventilation required

Minor Criteria (≥3 = ICU)

  • PaO2/FiO2 ≤250
  • Multilobar infiltrates
  • Confusion

ATS/IDSA 2019 Guidelines

Outpatient, No Comorbidities

  • Amoxicillin 1g PO TID
  • OR Doxycycline 100mg BID
  • OR Azithromycin 500mg day 1 → 250mg days 2–5 (only if local macrolide resistance <25%)

Outpatient with Comorbidities (COPD, DM, CKD, CHF, EtOH, Malignancy)

  • Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 BID + macrolide
  • OR Respiratory fluoroquinolone monotherapy: levofloxacin 750mg daily or moxifloxacin 400mg daily

Inpatient, Non-ICU

  • Beta-lactam (ampicillin-sulbactam 3g IV q6h, ceftriaxone 1–2g IV daily) + macrolide (azithromycin 500mg IV/PO daily)
  • OR Respiratory FQ monotherapy

ICU

  • Beta-lactam + macrolide (preferred over beta-lactam + FQ)
  • Add vancomycin 15–20 mg/kg IV q8–12h or linezolid 600mg IV q12h if MRSA risk
  • Add anti-pseudomonal if risk factors (structural lung disease, prior culture)
MRSA nasal screen negative → NPV >95%, safe to de-escalate MRSA coverage. This is a powerful tool to avoid unnecessary vancomycin.

Adjunctive Steroids — CAPE COD Trial (2023 NEJM)

Dexamethasone 6mg IV daily × 4 days in severe CAP (PSI ≥III). Reduced 28-day mortality from 9.3% to 6.2% (NNT ~32). NOT for influenza, fungal, or PJP.

Duration

  • Minimum 5 days, clinically stable ≥48h before stopping
  • Most patients: 5–7 days total
  • Follow-up CXR at 6 weeks in smokers >50 to rule out underlying malignancy

Hospital-Acquired & Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia

Nosocomial pneumonia from MDR organisms; HAP ≥48h after admission, VAP ≥48h after intubation.
MRSA + Pseudomonas De-escalate at 48–72h 7-Day Course #1 cause of death among HAIs; 13% attributable mortality

Shift in Oropharyngeal Flora

Within 48 hours of hospitalization, oropharyngeal flora shifts from normal commensals to gram-negative bacilli and MRSA due to:

  • Mucosal injury from intubation
  • Antibiotic suppression of normal flora
  • Reduced gastric acidity from PPIs
  • Impaired local immunity from critical illness

Mechanism

Primary mechanism: microaspiration around ET tube cuff. Even high-volume low-pressure (HVLP) cuffs allow passage through longitudinal folds. Pooled subglottic secretions above the cuff are a major reservoir → subglottic suctioning ET tubes reduce VAP incidence by ~50%.

Biofilm formation on ET tube within 24 hours. Impaired mucociliary clearance. Impaired cough reflex from sedation.

Gastric colonization: PPI allows bacterial overgrowth → retrograde oropharyngeal colonization → aspiration.

Result: multifocal bronchopneumonia (not lobar).

Clinical Features

HAP/VAP presents as new or progressive radiographic infiltrate PLUS ≥2 of:

  • Fever >38°C or hypothermia <36°C
  • Leukocytosis >12,000 or leukopenia <4,000
  • Purulent secretions (change in sputum color/volume)
  • Worsening oxygenation (increasing FiO2/PEEP requirements)
VAP is a clinical diagnosis. No single test is definitive. The Clinical Pulmonary Infection Score (CPIS) was developed to standardize diagnosis but has modest accuracy. Culture data is essential for guiding therapy, not for making the diagnosis.

Diagnostic Workup

  • CXR: New or progressive infiltrate (multifocal bronchopneumonia pattern typical)
  • Respiratory cultures: Endotracheal aspirate (semi-quantitative) or BAL (>104 CFU/mL = positive)
  • Blood cultures x2: Before starting antibiotics
  • MRSA nasal screen: If negative, NPV >95% for MRSA pneumonia → withhold/stop vancomycin
  • Procalcitonin: Useful for de-escalation (serial trending)
ATS/IDSA 2016 recommends noninvasive sampling (endotracheal aspirate) over invasive (BAL) for initial workup. Invasive sampling does not improve mortality but may reduce antibiotic use.

Empiric Antibiotic Selection (ATS/IDSA 2016)

Low MDR Risk (No risk factors, ≤5 days hospitalized)

Single anti-pseudomonal agent (choose one):

  • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g IV q6h
  • Cefepime 2g IV q8h
  • Levofloxacin 750mg IV daily
  • Meropenem 1g IV q8h

High MDR Risk (risk factors present or >10–20% local MRSA/MDR-GNR prevalence)

Two anti-pseudomonal agents from different classes + MRSA coverage:

  • Vancomycin (target AUC/MIC 400–600) OR linezolid 600mg IV q12h
  • PLUS two different anti-pseudomonals (e.g., pip-tazo + ciprofloxacin; cefepime + tobramycin)
De-escalate at 48–72h based on culture results. This is mandatory, not optional. Duration: 7 days (Chastre et al., JAMA 2003: 8 vs 15 days showed no difference in outcomes except for non-fermenters like Pseudomonas). Procalcitonin-guided discontinuation is safe and reduces antibiotic exposure.

Prognosis

  • HAP crude mortality: 20–50%
  • VAP attributable mortality: ~13% (many patients die WITH VAP, not purely FROM it)
  • VAP increases ICU LOS by 4–13 days
  • Inappropriate initial antibiotics increase mortality by 2–3x
1
Know your antibiogram. Local resistance patterns drive empiric therapy. If >20% MRSA prevalence, add vancomycin/linezolid empirically.
2
Stenotrophomonas maltophilia is intrinsically resistant to carbapenems. Treat with TMP-SMX. Often emerges after prolonged carbapenem use.
3
Ventilator-associated tracheobronchitis (VAT) — purulent secretions + positive culture but NO new infiltrate on CXR. Controversial whether to treat; not the same as VAP.

Aspiration Pneumonia

Pulmonary infection from aspiration of oropharyngeal/gastric contents. Critical distinction: pneumonitis (sterile acid) vs pneumonia (infected oral flora).
RLL Dependent Segments Anaerobes Aspiration ≠ Always Abx 20–30% in hospitalized patients

Pneumonitis vs Pneumonia — The Critical Distinction

FeatureAspiration PneumonitisAspiration Pneumonia
MechanismChemical acid burn (sterile)Bacterial infection from oral flora
OnsetMinutes to hoursHours to days
CXRBilateral diffuse infiltratesFocal dependent segment consolidation
Antibiotics?Usually NOYES
CourseOften improves in 24–48hWorsens without treatment

Risk Factors

  • Altered mental status — #1 risk factor (EtOH, seizure, sedation, stroke, anesthesia)
  • Dysphagia — stroke, Parkinson disease, myasthenia gravis, ALS
  • GERD
  • Poor dental hygiene — increases anaerobic bacterial load in mouth
  • Alcoholism
  • PEG tubes — do NOT prevent aspiration (common misconception)

Clinical Features

  • Cough, fever, purulent sputum in a patient with aspiration risk factors
  • May be indolent (days to weeks if anaerobic)
  • Putrid/foul-smelling sputum = highly suggestive of anaerobic infection (~60%)
  • Often preceded by witnessed aspiration event, altered consciousness, or dysphagia

Location

  • Upright: Right lower lobe (posterior basal segment)
  • Supine: Superior segment of RLL or posterior segment of RUL
  • Right side predominates because right main bronchus is wider, shorter, and more vertical

Imaging

  • CXR: Infiltrate in dependent lung segments
  • CT: More sensitive; may show early cavitation, necrotizing pneumonia
  • Pleural effusion common (empyema if complicated)

Microbiology

Polymicrobial in most cases. Anaerobes (Peptostreptococcus, Fusobacterium, Prevotella, Bacteroides) + aerobes (streptococci). Anaerobic cultures are often not sent (difficult to obtain uncontaminated specimen).

Diagnosis is largely clinical — patient with aspiration risk factors + infiltrate in dependent segment. Culture rarely changes management.

Antibiotic Therapy

First-Line

  • Ampicillin-sulbactam 3g IV q6h (covers anaerobes + common aerobes)
  • OR Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 BID (outpatient)

PCN-Allergic

  • Clindamycin 600mg IV q8h → 300mg PO QID

Duration

  • 7 days for uncomplicated aspiration pneumonia
  • 4–6 weeks if lung abscess develops

Prevention

StrategyEvidence
HOB elevation 30–45°Reduces aspiration risk in hospitalized patients
NPO until swallow eval post-strokeStandard of care; speech pathology consult
Oral hygieneReduces aspiration PNA by 40% in nursing home patients
Minimize sedationPreserves cough and gag reflexes

Prognosis

  • Hospitalized aspiration pneumonia: 20–30% mortality
  • Higher mortality in elderly, debilitated, recurrent aspiration
  • Aspiration pneumonitis usually self-resolves in 24–48h
1
“Witnessed aspiration” ≠ “needs antibiotics.” Most witnessed events cause pneumonitis (sterile chemical burn). Add antibiotics only if no improvement at 48h, or if the patient develops fever/leukocytosis/purulent sputum.
2
PEG tubes do NOT reduce aspiration risk. This is one of the most common misconceptions. PEG tubes prevent starvation, not aspiration.
3
Putrid sputum = anaerobic infection (volatile fatty acids from anaerobic metabolism). This finding is essentially pathognomonic.

Atypical Pneumonia

Organisms not visible on Gram stain, insidious onset, dry cough, extrapulmonary symptoms — “looks better than the CXR.”
Walking Pneumonia Bilateral Interstitial Macrolide / Doxycycline 20–40% of CAP in ambulatory patients

Mycoplasma pneumoniae

  • Ages 5–40 (most common atypical; school-age children and young adults)
  • Lacks a cell wall → beta-lactams are useless (no target for penicillin-binding proteins)
  • P1 adhesin protein binds to respiratory epithelium → ciliostasis → impaired mucociliary clearance
  • Cold agglutinins (IgM against I antigen on RBCs, present in ~50%) → can cause hemolytic anemia
  • Predominantly causes interstitial pneumonia (inflammation around alveolar septa, not filling alveoli)

Legionella pneumophila

  • Contaminated water sources — cooling towers, hot tubs, cruise ship water systems, hospital plumbing
  • Intracellular pathogen — lives inside macrophages. Prevents phagosome-lysosome fusion (similar to TB)
  • NOT transmitted person-to-person
  • Can cause severe pneumonia requiring ICU (unlike Mycoplasma)

Chlamydia pneumoniae

  • Obligate intracellular organism with unique lifecycle (elementary body = infectious; reticulate body = replicating)
  • Transmitted person-to-person via respiratory droplets
  • Causes mild-moderate pneumonia; rarely severe

Mycoplasma pneumoniae

  • Dry, hacking cough (hallmark) — persistent, annoying, non-productive
  • Low-grade fever, malaise, headache
  • Extrapulmonary manifestations:
    • Bullous myringitis — hemorrhagic blisters on tympanic membrane (rare but classic board buzzword)
    • Erythema multiforme / Stevens-Johnson Syndrome
    • Hemolytic anemia (cold agglutinins)
    • Guillain-Barré syndrome
    • Arthralgias, pericarditis, encephalitis

Legionella pneumophila

  • High fever + relative bradycardia (pulse-temperature dissociation)
  • GI symptoms — diarrhea in 20–50% (unusual for pneumonia)
  • Lab cluster: hyponatremia, elevated LFTs, elevated CK
  • Gram stain: many neutrophils but NO organisms visible (intracellular)
  • Can progress to severe pneumonia, respiratory failure, multi-organ dysfunction

Chlamydia pneumoniae

  • Biphasic illness: pharyngitis first → pneumonia 1–3 weeks later
  • Hoarseness = most characteristic feature (laryngitis)
  • Wheezing (bronchospasm)
Board Buzzwords: Bullous myringitis = Mycoplasma • Relative bradycardia + diarrhea + hyponatremia = Legionella • Biphasic + hoarseness = Chlamydia

Imaging

CXR typically shows bilateral interstitial/reticulonodular infiltrates that look worse than the patient appears clinically (“CXR-clinical dissociation”).

Legionella is a reportable disease. Notify public health for source investigation (cooling towers, water systems).

Prognosis

  • Mycoplasma: Self-limited in most; mortality <1%. Rarely causes ARDS in adults
  • Legionella: Mortality 5–30%, especially if immunocompromised or delayed treatment
  • Chlamydia: Generally mild; mortality very low
1
Mycoplasma is the #1 cause of pneumonia in ages 5–40. In a young, healthy adult with dry cough and bilateral interstitial infiltrates → think Mycoplasma first.
2
Legionella can be severe. Unlike other atypicals, Legionella frequently requires ICU care and has significant mortality. It behaves more like a “typical” severe pneumonia in its clinical course.
3
Cold agglutinin test: Place blood tube on ice — if RBCs clump and then disaggregate upon warming, cold agglutinins are present. This is a bedside test for Mycoplasma.
4
Pontiac fever = mild, self-limited form of Legionella infection (flu-like illness without pneumonia). Same organism, different host response.

PJP (Pneumocystis jirovecii Pneumonia)

Opportunistic fungal pneumonia from P. jirovecii in CD4 <200 or immunosuppressed patients.
CD4 <200 Ground-Glass Opacities TMP-SMX + Steroids if Hypoxic 10–20% (HIV) | 30–60% (non-HIV)

Organism & Mechanism

P. jirovecii is an atypical fungus — classified as a fungus by ribosomal RNA but behaves clinically like neither a typical fungus nor a bacterium.

  • Cell wall contains beta-D-glucan but NO ergosterol → amphotericin B and azoles are useless
  • Trophic form attaches to type I pneumocytes
  • Creates foamy eosinophilic exudate in alveoli that forms a diffusion barrier → progressive hypoxemia
  • Impaired gas exchange → exertional desaturation (classic presentation)

Why Non-HIV PJP is WORSE (Counterintuitive)

Non-HIV immunosuppressed patients (transplant, chemo, chronic steroids) mount a more intense inflammatory response than HIV patients. HIV patients have low CD4 counts → less inflammation → slower disease. Non-HIV patients retain enough immune function to create severe inflammation → faster respiratory failure → higher mortality (30–60% vs 10–20%).

Clinical Features

  • Subacute onset over days to weeks (not acute like bacterial pneumonia)
  • Progressive dyspnea — the cardinal symptom
  • Dry, nonproductive cough
  • Exertional desaturation — SpO2 95% at rest drops to 85% with ambulation (classic finding)
  • Clear lungs on auscultation with terrible ABG (CXR-exam dissociation)
  • Low-grade fever
Classic PJP presentation: HIV patient with CD4 <200 + subacute dyspnea + dry cough + bilateral GGO + clear lung exam + exertional desaturation

Laboratory Findings

TestFindingClinical Utility
LDH>500 U/L95% sensitive but NOT specific. Normal LDH virtually rules out PJP.

ABGElevated A-a gradientUsed to determine steroid eligibility (A-a >35 or PaO2 <70)

Imaging

  • CXR: Bilateral, symmetric, perihilar ground-glass opacities (GGO). May be normal early.
  • CT: Ground-glass opacities + crazy-paving pattern. Apical sparing if on aerosolized pentamidine. Cystic changes (risk for pneumothorax).

Definitive Diagnosis

  • Induced sputum with GMS (Gomori methenamine silver) or DFA staining — 50–90% sensitivity
  • BAL (bronchoalveolar lavage) — >95% sensitivity, gold standard
  • “Crushed ping-pong balls” on silver stain = classic description of PJP cysts
P. jirovecii cannot be cultured. Diagnosis requires visualization on stain or PCR detection.

Treatment

First-Line

TMP-SMX 15–20 mg/kg/day (TMP component) divided q6–8h × 21 days

(For a 70kg patient: ~5 DS tablets/day or IV equivalent)

Adjunctive Corticosteroids (If Hypoxic)

Indications: PaO2 <70 mmHg or A-a gradient >35 on room air
Regimen: Prednisone 40mg BID × 5 days → 40mg daily × 5 days → 20mg daily × 11 days
Must start within 72h of TMP-SMX (Bozzette et al., NEJM 1990). Reduces mortality from ~40% to ~20% in moderate-severe PJP.
Mechanism: Blunts paradoxical worsening from immune reconstitution when organisms are killed.

Alternatives (if TMP-SMX intolerant)

Prophylaxis

  • TMP-SMX SS daily or DS 3x/week when CD4 <200
  • Discontinue when CD4 >200 for ≥3 months on ART
  • Alternatives: dapsone, atovaquone, aerosolized pentamidine (monthly)

1
Pneumothorax in an HIV patient → think PJP cystic changes. PJP creates subpleural cysts that rupture. Bilateral pneumothorax is classic.
2
TMP-SMX prophylaxis also covers Toxoplasma. Two-for-one protection in HIV patients.
3
Non-HIV PJP is worse because the intact (but suppressed) immune system creates more inflammation. Counterintuitive but critical to remember.
4
LDH as a screening tool: Normal LDH (<250) has excellent NPV for PJP. Use it to risk-stratify dyspneic HIV patients.

Lung Abscess

Necrotizing pulmonary infection forming a thick-walled cavity with air-fluid level, most commonly from aspiration of anaerobes.
Putrid Sputum Air-Fluid Level 4–6 Weeks Antibiotics 5–10% primary | 20–50% secondary

Mechanism

Aspiration → pneumonia → necrosis (anaerobic enzymes) → cavitation → wall formation.

  • Primary (80%): Aspiration-related. Anaerobic bacteria from gingival crevices (Peptostreptococcus, Fusobacterium, Prevotella, Bacteroides)
  • Secondary (20%): Obstruction (lung cancer, foreign body) or hematogenous seeding (septic emboli from endocarditis, Lemierre syndrome)

Anaerobic organisms produce proteolytic enzymes that cause tissue necrosis. As necrotic debris liquefies and is expectorated (or drains into a bronchus), a cavity with an air-fluid level forms.

Clinical Features

  • Indolent course over weeks (not acute)
  • Foul/putrid sputum — pathognomonic for anaerobes (~60%); caused by volatile fatty acids
  • Hemoptysis — 30–50%
  • Night sweats, weight loss — mimics TB and lung cancer
  • Poor dentition on exam — source of anaerobes
The clinical triad of indolent course + putrid sputum + poor dentition + dependent segment cavity is classic for primary lung abscess from anaerobic aspiration.

Imaging

  • CXR: Thick-walled cavity (>4mm wall) with air-fluid level in dependent lung segments
  • CT: Better characterization of cavity wall, surrounding consolidation, and relationship to pleura

Antibiotic Therapy

First-Line Options

  • Clindamycin 600mg IV q8h → 300mg PO QID
  • OR Ampicillin-sulbactam 3g IV q6h
  • OR Amoxicillin-clavulanate (outpatient step-down)

Duration

4–6 weeks minimum. Treat until CXR shows resolution or stable small residual cavity. Premature discontinuation leads to relapse.

Interventional / Surgical

  • CT-guided percutaneous drainage: If >6 cm or failing antibiotics at 7–10 days
  • Bronchoscopy: If atypical location (non-dependent segment) to rule out obstructing cancer or foreign body
  • Surgical resection: Rare; for massive hemoptysis, failed drainage, or suspected malignancy

Prognosis

TypeMortality
Primary (aspiration-related)5–10%

1
Putrid sputum = anaerobic infection. The foul smell comes from volatile fatty acids produced by anaerobic metabolism. This sign is essentially pathognomonic.
2
Non-dependent segment abscess + smoker >50 = mandatory bronchoscopy to rule out obstructing lung cancer causing post-obstructive abscess.
3
IVDU + bilateral cavitating nodules = septic emboli from tricuspid valve endocarditis (S. aureus). Not a true lung abscess but an important differential.
4
Lemierre syndrome: Fusobacterium necrophorum pharyngitis → internal jugular vein thrombophlebitis → septic pulmonary emboli with cavitating nodules. Young adults after pharyngitis.

The CURB-65 score (Lim et al., Thorax 2003) is a validated bedside tool for assessing severity in community-acquired pneumonia and guiding disposition. Each criterion scores 1 point (total 0–5).
